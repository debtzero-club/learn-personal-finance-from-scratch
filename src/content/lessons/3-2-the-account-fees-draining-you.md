## The situation

You glance at your statement and see a $12 charge you don't recognize. It's not a purchase — it's a "monthly maintenance fee." You didn't sign up for it on purpose. It just appears, every month, like rent on your own money.

## The idea

Banks have a menu of small fees that are easy to miss because each one is small. Stacked over a year, they're not small at all. The three that hit beginners most:

- **Monthly maintenance fee.** A flat charge just for having the account — often $5 to $15 a month. Many banks waive it *if* you keep a minimum balance or have direct deposit, but if you don't qualify, you pay.
- **ATM fees.** Use an ATM outside your bank's network and you can get charged twice: once by the ATM owner, once by your own bank. Two fees for one withdrawal.
- **Minimum-balance fee.** Drop below a required balance — say $500 — even for a day, and you get dinged.

Here's the part nobody tells you: **none of these are mandatory.** Plenty of online banks and credit unions offer checking with **no monthly fee, no minimum balance, and a big free ATM network.** A fee isn't the cost of having a bank account — it's the cost of having the *wrong* account.

**The rule of thumb: you should be paying $0 a month to hold a checking account.** If you're paying more, the account — not your behavior — is the problem.

## By the numbers

Let's price out one common combo for someone earning around the $48,000 single-filer salary we use throughout this course (roughly $1,567 per biweekly paycheck after taxes):

| Fee | Each | Per year |
|-----|------|----------|
| Monthly maintenance | $12 × 12 | **$144** |
| Out-of-network ATM | $3.50 × 24 | **$84** |
| One minimum-balance slip | $25 × 2 | **$50** |
| **Total** | | **$278/yr** |

That's nearly a fifth of a paycheck — gone, every year, to fees you can opt out of by switching to a fee-free account. Setting up a no-fee account takes an afternoon and pays you back $278 a year, every year. Few money moves are that cheap or that permanent.
