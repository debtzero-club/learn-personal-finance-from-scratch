## The situation

Your credit score gets all the attention, but it's just a number squeezed out of a bigger document you've probably never read: your **credit report.** And here's the unsettling part — credit reports contain errors surprisingly often, and an error you don't catch can quietly cost you a loan approval or a better rate. The fix is free, and it starts with simply *looking.*

## The idea

Your **credit report** is the detailed file the credit bureaus keep on your borrowing: every account, its balance and limit, your payment history, and certain public records. There are three major bureaus, and each keeps its own report — so it's worth checking all three, since they don't always match. Your score (lesson 7.7) is calculated *from* this report, which is why the report is the thing that actually matters.

**You're entitled to these reports for free.** The one official, federally authorized source is **AnnualCreditReport.com.** This is important: there are lookalike sites designed to charge you or sign you up for subscriptions for something you can get free. Use the real one. (The Federal Trade Commission, which helps oversee this, points to the same official site.)

When you pull a report, **read it like a detective:**

- **Accounts you don't recognize** — a card or loan you never opened can be a sign of identity theft.
- **Wrong balances or limits** — these can distort your utilization and your score.
- **Late payments you didn't make** — a misreported late mark is a common, fixable error.
- **Old negative items** — most negatives should age off after about seven years; check that they have.

If you find something wrong, **you have the right to dispute it, for free.** You contact the credit bureau (and the company that reported the information), and the bureau **must investigate** — generally within about 30 days — and correct or delete anything that's inaccurate or can't be verified.

**The rule of thumb: pull your reports free at AnnualCreditReport.com, read them for errors, and dispute anything wrong — it's a free way to protect your score.** A blank score isn't the only risk; a *wrong* report is just as costly, and just as fixable.

## By the numbers

Here's why a single error matters. Imagine our cardholder's report wrongly shows a credit card with a much lower limit than they actually have:

| What the report shows | Reported limit | Their $1,500 balance reads as | Effect |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Correct | $5,000 | 30% utilization | Healthy |
| Error (limit understated) | $2,000 | 75% utilization | Drags the score down |

Same real balance, same person — but a single wrong number on the report makes their utilization *look* far worse, and the score suffers for a mistake that isn't theirs. That's the cost of not looking: an error you never see still hurts you. Disputing it costs nothing but a little time, and the bureau has to investigate. Checking your report once or twice a year is one of the highest-value, lowest-effort financial habits there is.

## Where this fits

Your report is the source of truth your score is built on, and you get it free — so check it, read it for errors, and dispute anything wrong.
