## The situation

A text lands: "ALERT: unusual activity on your account. Verify immediately or it will be locked," with a link. Your stomach drops and your thumb hovers over the link. That spike of panic is the entire point — scams are engineered to make you act before you think. Knowing the playbook turns that panic into a shrug.

## The idea

Keeping your money safe comes down to three things: **spot the scam, lock the account, and know what to do if you're hit.**

### 1. Spot the scams

The most common ones aimed at beginners all share a fingerprint — **urgency plus a request for information or money:**

- **Impersonation.** Someone pretends to be your bank, the IRS, a utility, or even a "boss" or "family member" in trouble. Real institutions don't cold-call demanding payment or passwords.
- **Phishing texts and emails.** A fake "your account is locked / package failed / payment declined" message with a link to a lookalike login page that steals what you type.
- **"Verify your code" calls.** A caller asks you to read back the code that was just texted to you. That code is your security key — handing it over hands them your account.
- **Fake check / overpayment.** Someone "accidentally" overpays you and asks you to send the difference back. Their check bounces later; your real money is gone.

**The rule of thumb: no real bank, agency, or company will ever ask for your password, full card number, or a one-time code — and anyone rushing you is rushing you for a reason.** When in doubt, hang up and contact the company yourself using the number on your card or app, never the one they gave you.

### 2. Lock the account with 2FA

**Two-factor authentication (2FA)** adds a second step to logging in — usually a code from an app or a tap on your phone — on top of your password. It matters because passwords leak constantly in data breaches; 2FA means **a stolen password alone isn't enough to get in.** Turn it on for your bank and your email first (your email is the master key that can reset everything else). Where you have the choice, use an **authenticator app** rather than text-message codes — texts can be intercepted, app codes can't.

### 3. Know what to do if you're hit

Speed limits the damage. If you suspect fraud or identity theft:

1. **Call your bank or card issuer right away** (the number on the back of the card) to freeze or replace the card and dispute the charges.
2. **Freeze your credit** with the three credit bureaus — it's **free**, blocks new accounts from being opened in your name, and you can lift it anytime.
3. **Report it.** The official US site for an identity-theft recovery plan is **IdentityTheft.gov**, and your bank and the CFPB can guide you through disputing unauthorized charges.
4. **Change passwords** on the affected accounts (and any account that shared that password), and turn on 2FA if it wasn't already.

## By the numbers

There's no dollar table here — the math of safety is simpler than that. **2FA takes about two minutes to switch on. A credit freeze is free and reversible. Reporting fast can be the difference between a canceled card and months of cleanup.** Set up the protections now, while nothing's wrong, so that the day a scary text arrives, you already know it's just a text — and your accounts are locked tight behind a second key.
