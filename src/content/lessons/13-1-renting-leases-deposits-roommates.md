## The situation

You found the place. The landlord slides a 14-page lease across the table and says "standard stuff, just sign the last page." Your roommate-to-be is already measuring for a couch. This is the moment most renting problems are born — because everything that goes wrong six months from now (the deposit fight, the surprise utility bill, the roommate who ghosts on rent) is usually decided right here, before you've spent a single night in the place.

## The idea

Three things to get right, in order:

### The lease is a contract — read it before you sign it

A **lease** is a legally binding agreement, not paperwork theater. Before signing, find these five things in it:

- **The term** — how long you're committed (usually 12 months) and what breaking it early costs.
- **The rent** — the amount, the due date, and the late fee.
- **What breaks it** — the rules that can get you evicted or charged (unauthorized pets, smoking, unapproved roommates).
- **Who pays utilities** — electric, gas, water, trash, internet. "Rent: $1,400" tells you nothing about these.
- **Guest and sublet rules** — can a partner stay over regularly? Can you hand your room to someone else if you move?

HUD's tenant-rights resources are the place to learn what protections your state adds on top — every state's rules differ.

### The security deposit is your money — protect it with a camera

A **security deposit** (often about one month's rent) is money the landlord holds against damage or unpaid rent. State law typically caps how much can be charged and sets rules for returning it — minus *documented* damage beyond normal wear and tear. The key word is documented: **the move-in photo album is what gets your deposit back.** No photos means no proof of which dents were already there.

### One lease, every name, all the rent

If you and your roommates sign one lease together, you usually have **joint liability**: the landlord can collect the *entire* rent from *any one of you*. A roommate who skips their half isn't just their problem — it's legally yours. **The rule of thumb: put every money agreement between roommates in writing** — who pays what, by when, and what happens if someone leaves early. A shared note beats a friendship-ending argument.

## By the numbers

The advertised rent is never the real number. Here's the true monthly cost of a hypothetical **$1,400/month apartment split two ways** — illustrative round numbers, not quotes:

| Monthly cost (your share) | Amount |
| --- | ---: |
| Rent ($1,400 ÷ 2) | $700 |
| Utilities + internet (~$180 ÷ 2) | $90 |
| Renters insurance (illustrative, ~$10–20/mo — your own policy, lesson 12.3) | ~$15 |
| **True monthly cost** | **~$805** |

That's about **15% more than the $700** you had in your head — every month. And move-in week costs more still: first month's rent plus the security deposit (often another full month, so ~$700 more for your half) due before you get keys.

Where does the $805 live in your budget? It's the biggest line in your 50/30/20 **needs** bucket from lesson 4.2 — which is exactly why you price the *true* cost, not the advertised rent, before you say yes to a place.
