## The situation

"I really need to start saving." You've said it. Maybe you meant it. And then three months passed and the balance looked about the same. That's not a willpower failure — it's a goal-design failure. "Start saving" gives your brain nothing to grab onto.

## The idea

A goal you'll actually hit has three parts:

1. **A number.** Not "more." A specific dollar amount.
2. **A deadline.** A date gives the number urgency and lets you check if you're on track.
3. **A first step small enough that skipping it feels silly.** This is the secret. Most goals die at step one because step one is too big.

Put together, the shape is: **"$____ for ____ by ____."**

- "$1,000 emergency fund by next March."
- "$500 to cover holiday gifts by December."
- "$2,000 toward my credit card by the end of the year."

Then break the number into an automatic, boring first step. $1,000 in ten months is **$25 a week**. You don't have to *feel motivated* every week — you set up the transfer once (Phase 4 covers exactly how) and let it run.

## By the numbers

Watch how a deadline turns a fuzzy goal into a plan:

| Goal | Amount | Deadline | What it actually means |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| "Save more" | ??? | someday | nothing happens |
| Emergency fund | $1,000 | 10 months | $25 / week |
| Emergency fund | $1,000 | 5 months | $50 / week |

Same goal, two timelines, two very different weekly numbers — and now you can pick the one that fits your paycheck instead of guessing.

## Pick one, not five

You might have a dozen money goals. For now, choose **one**. Beginners who chase five goals at once usually finish zero. One clear goal, one automatic transfer, one deadline. Hit it, feel the win, then set the next. Momentum is the real asset you're building here.
