## The situation

You open a banking app, a benefits portal, or a credit card statement, and within ten seconds there are six numbers, three acronyms, and a word you've never seen. The feeling that follows — "I should understand this and I don't" — is incredibly common. It is not a sign that something's wrong with you.

## The idea

Two things make money feel harder than it is:

**1. You were probably never taught it.** Personal finance still isn't a guaranteed class in US schools. Most adults walked into their first job knowing the quadratic formula but not how to read a pay stub. That's a curriculum problem, not an intelligence problem.

**2. Some of it is confusing on purpose.** Clarity isn't always in the seller's interest. A credit card that clearly said "if you only pay the minimum, this $1,000 will cost you years and hundreds in interest" would get used differently. Fine print, surprise fees, and jargon aren't accidents — they're a business model. Once you can name that, you stop blaming yourself and start reading more carefully.

The good news: the actual *core* of personal finance is small. It's maybe a dozen ideas — spend less than you earn, keep a cushion, understand interest, don't carry high-interest debt, grab free money at work. Everything else is detail. This course just walks you through those ideas one at a time.

## By the numbers

Surveys of US adults consistently find that a majority can't answer a few basic money questions (about interest, inflation, and risk) — and that the people who *feel* least confident are often the ones who were simply never shown. Confidence, it turns out, predicts good money behavior almost as much as knowledge does. So part of the job here is just building the feeling of "okay, I can handle this" — because you can.

## The reframe

For the rest of this course, try swapping the thought *"I'm bad with money"* for *"I haven't learned this part yet."* The first one is a dead end. The second one is just a to-do list — and you're already working through it.
