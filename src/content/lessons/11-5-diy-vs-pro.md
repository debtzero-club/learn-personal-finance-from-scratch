# When to DIY vs. get a pro

> One W-2 plus the standard deduction is a free, one-hour DIY filing; side income, multiple states, or big life changes are when paying a professional starts paying for itself — match the tool to the complexity.

## The situation

Every spring, the ads arrive: filing is dangerous, the rules changed, you need protection, upgrade now. Meanwhile your actual tax year was one W-2 and a student loan. Before paying anyone — software upsell or human professional — it's worth knowing the honest answer to one question: *how complicated is my year, really?*

## The idea

The DIY-vs-pro decision is a **complexity test, not a courage test.** The same fork showed up at the end of lesson 2.6; here's the full decision frame.

**The free DIY lane.** If your year is one W-2 plus the standard deduction — maybe with one-box extras like student loan interest (11.1) or a little bank interest — free filing handles it. The IRS lists free ways to e-file (the first source below), and the walk-through is genuinely line by line. That covered the anchor's entire return in lesson 2.6, in about an hour.

**Free human help.** If you'd rather have a person do it and your income qualifies, **VITA** (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance) prepares returns for free with IRS-certified volunteers — many first-job incomes qualify. Free help, no upsell.

**When a pro starts earning the fee.** Each of these flags adds real rules, real deadlines, and real mistakes to avoid:

- **Side income / 1099s** — self-employment tax, deductible expenses, quarterly estimates (lesson 11.3)
- **Multiple states** — moving mid-year or working across state lines means part-year returns
- **Itemizing** — your deductions actually beat the standard amount (lesson 11.2's rare case)
- **Big life changes** — marriage, a home purchase, large investment sales

One flag is usually still software territory. Two or more — or one you genuinely don't understand — is when a professional's fee can be smaller than the errors and missed deductions it prevents. Hiring one isn't failing the test; it's reading your year correctly.

**The rule of thumb: match the tool to the complexity — never pay for complexity you don't have.**

## By the numbers

The decision frame as a table:

| Your tax year looks like | A sensible route |
| --- | --- |
| One W-2, standard deduction | **Free DIY** — about an hour, line by line |
| + student loan interest, a little bank interest | **Still free DIY** — these are one-box entries |
| Income-qualified and want human help | **VITA** — free, IRS-certified preparation |
| + side income / 1099s and quarterly estimates | Software can do it — but a pro starts to make sense |
| Multiple states, marriage, a home, big investment sales | **A pro** — or at least a one-time consult |

Note what's *not* in the table: brand names. Free or paid, judge any tool by what your year needs — not by which ad ran most.
